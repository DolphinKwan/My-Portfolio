ABSTRACT

The Anti-Cruelty Society website provides adoption services and various pet-related information. Our primary goal for this project is to improve the effectiveness of the website and the satisfaction of the users by:
1. optimizing the information architecture and 2. redesigning the filter feature and adoption-related pages.

In this study, we conducted a quantitative comparison study of the The Anti-Cruelty Society website with our redesigned high-fidelity prototype.

INFOMATION ARCHITECTURE REDESIGN

CARD SORT

Due to the large amount of content on the site, we decided to picked contents that are only relevant to our proposal for card sorting. because of the large amount of content on the site, we decided to simplify the information structure base on our previous analysis before we do any further user study.

There are Over 700 pages on the site and some of them can be restructured to a higher level so that we can focus on a smaller range

By reconstructing the information architecture We narrowed down our investigation to 7 categories and 38 sub-categories

The average agreement rate of Card Sorting was 25.7%, and none of the category agreement rates were higher than 40.0%

By reconstructing the information architecture We narrowed down our investigation to 7 categories and 38 sub-categories

Card Sorting Design Retrospection

One of the reasons why the agreement rate is so low is that participants did not know anything about the The Anti-cruelty Society. For example, participants put "spay and neuter" in the "pet care" category is because they don't know it's one of the main services of The Anti-Cruelty Society. If they know it they would place it into both "pet care" and "Our Services" categories.However, it doesn't mean the results of Card Sorting is not representative. We still got some valuable insights from it.

Moreover, participants provided a lot of category name suggestion which helped us refine the Level 1 navigation naming. For example, one participant created a category named “Services”. We combined it with the previous name “Our Works” to come up with “Our Services”.

TREE TESTING

In tree jack test, we tried to examine the rationality of the structure to the most extent, so our tasks were designed to cover most of the sections. They are:

  • 1. Home to home shelter bypass services
  • 2. Careers & internships
  • 3. Schedule an appointment now
  • 4. Report abuse
  • 5. Jumping up
  • 6. Low income services

The target pages are located into different sections

The average first-click success rate of the redesign structure in Tree Testing had increased by 13%

In the first round of the Tree Testing on the existing website, the average first-click success rate was 61%. In the second round of the Tree Testing on the redesigned menu, the average first-click success rate was improved to 74%

The average success rate of the redesign structure in Tree Testing had increased by 6%

In the first round of the Tree Testing, we provided 6 task for participants to test the existing website. We found the average success rate was 66%. In the second round, the success rate was 72%

The first-click success rate of task 3 in the Tree Testing had decreased by 14%
The success rate of task 3 in the Tree Testing had decreased by 40%
Tree Testing Design Retrospection

Task 3 (find the service of spay/neuter clinic) is the only task that we recorded a dramatic decrease on success rate. Our analysis suggest that it could be the tree test design flaws instead of a information architecture design failure. Without task 3, our test result will be more representative for improvement the rest of the tasks.

In the heuristic evaluation, we found that showing the Level 2 and Level 3 menus at the same time can cause a huge visual burden. 1 out of 6 participants in the existing website usability test also expressed the same concern. However, in the Tree Testing, the Level 2 and Level 3 menus were not shown as the original website did. We think this is the limitation of the Tree Testing.

Conclusion

In this project, our group redesigned The Anti-Cruelty Society website. Our goals were to help increase the effectiveness of the website menu labels and improve the subjective usability of the pet filter feature and accessibility to adoption-related pages. Through the heuristic evaluation, interviews, and usability test for the existing website, we found users’ pain points, behaviors, and needs. Based on the findings, we conducted Card Sorting and Tree Testing to improve the information architecture of the website. After that, we designed low-fidelity and high-fidelity prototypes and ran two rounds of usability tests to iterate and refine them.



By comparing the usability tests’ results between the existing website and our high-fidelity prototype, we achieved all of our goals shown by the prominent change in the percentages of effectiveness and satisfaction. The SUS score increased by 34.4 points (63%), the average task completion time of usability tests decreased by 68%, the number of errors and inventions decreased by 90%, and the average success rate of the usability test with a high-fidelity test increased by 10%.



We had 7 findings in total. These findings helped us optimize the adoption process, information architecture, the feature of the filter, and adoption-related information on the website.



Although our findings were mostly positive, there were some limitations in our study. For example, the capability of our prototyping tool restricted the functionality of our redesigned filter, and the demography diversity of our participants skew our test results. These limitations also provided us insights for improving the website in the future.



In the future, we will recruit diverse participants for our interviews and other tasks. We will also provide our findings to The Anti-Cruelty Society to gather their feedback and keep optimizing the website to make it more user-friendly.
